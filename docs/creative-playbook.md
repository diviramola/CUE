## PATTERNS THAT DRIVE VIDEO COMPLETION

### Pattern C1: Open with Recognition, Not Brand

**What:** Delay brand reveal. Open with a moment the viewer recognizes from their own life, a visual surprise, or a curiosity gap — never a logo.
**Metric:** Video completion
**Evidence:** 10 of 13 ads delay brand reveal past the 20-second mark. 8 of 13 use end-card-only logo placement. The 3 ads with early brand (ad_003 WD-40, ad_008 Colgate, ad_009 Sony LIV) use brand-as-part-of-the-joke — brand visibility IS the creative mechanic.
**Confidence:** Strong

**Why it works:** A logo in the first frame signals "this is an ad" and triggers skip/scroll instinct. A relatable moment, surprise, or mystery activates curiosity circuits before the rational ad-filter engages. By the time the brand appears, the viewer is emotionally invested.

**Examples:**
- ad_010 (Adani): Brand at 50s in a 60s ad. Mystery catchphrase keeps you watching to solve the puzzle.
- ad_007 (Cadbury): Brand at 55s. The emotional narrative is so complete that the brand feels like a gentle signature, not an interruption.
- ad_012 (Surf Excel): Brand at 35s. The parenting story earns 35 seconds of attention before asking you to care about detergent.

**Counter-example (when early brand WORKS):**
- ad_008 (Colgate): Brand at 2s — but the INVERSION depends on knowing it is Colgate. "Why is a toothpaste brand selling food?" only works if you see Colgate first. Early brand works ONLY when the brand identity is essential to the creative surprise.

**How to apply:** Open Wiom ads with a family scenario (video call freezing, kids struggling with homework, cricket stream buffering). Let the problem earn attention. Wiom brand appears at the solution moment, not before.

### Pattern C2: The Curiosity Engine — Open Loops & Escalation

**What:** Create an unanswered question or escalating absurdity that the viewer MUST resolve by watching.
**Metric:** Video completion
**Evidence:** 9 of 13 ads use an explicit curiosity/retention mechanic — either an open question (ad_007 Cadbury, ad_010 Adani, ad_009 Sony LIV), escalation ladder (ad_002 Lahori Zeera, ad_003 WD-40), or debate/tension (ad_013 Tata Tea, ad_012 Surf Excel).
**Confidence:** Strong

**Why it works:** The brain cannot tolerate open loops (Zeigarnik Effect). An unanswered question creates cognitive tension that only resolves by continuing to watch. Escalation works similarly — each level predicts the next will be bigger, pulling the viewer forward.

**Two sub-patterns:**

**C2a: Mystery/Open Loop** (ad_010, ad_007, ad_009)
Open with something that does not make sense. The viewer stays to understand.
- Adani: "Fans before electricity" — nonsensical until the reveal.
- Sony LIV: "Do Not Apply" for a registration ad — contradictory.
- Cadbury: Woman pausing to rethink a story — why?

**C2b: Escalation Ladder** (ad_002, ad_003, ad_011)
Each scene is bigger/funnier/more extreme than the last.
- Lahori Zeera: Activities get more extreme with each cut.
- WD-40: Eight different use-cases, each its own mini-story.
- Meesho: Product categories pile up with escalating disbelief.

**How to apply:** Wiom could use either sub-pattern:
- Mystery: "Pehle phone charge karo, phir internet aayega" — a paradoxical catchphrase that resolves into Wiom's value prop.
- Escalation: A family's internet problems getting progressively more absurd (video call to the boss freezes, then the online exam crashes, then the wedding livestream drops) — each worse than the last, building to Wiom as the solution.

### Pattern C3: 30 Seconds is the Sweet Spot (for Digital)

**What:** 30-second ads dominate the library for digital formats. 60s+ works only with exceptional writing/talent.
**Metric:** Video completion
**Evidence:** 8 of 13 ads are 30 seconds. The 4 ads at 60s+ (Zomato, Cadbury, Adani, Tata Tea) all require either A-list talent (Piyush Mishra), deeply emotional narrative (Cadbury), mystery structure (Adani), or social-debate format (Tata Tea). The 6s format (WD-40) works specifically as non-skippable pre-roll.
**Confidence:** Moderate

**Why it works:** 30 seconds is long enough for problem-solution-CTA but short enough that most viewers reach the end. 60s demands either extraordinary craft or a retention mechanic strong enough to survive the 15-second attention cliff.

**How to apply:** Default to 30s for Wiom's digital ads. Only go 60s if the creative concept truly demands it AND you have a strong retention mechanic (mystery, debate, celebrity performance). Consider 6s bumper series for awareness/frequency plays.

---

### Pattern C4: Pattern Interrupts at the Midpoint

**What:** Top ads place a tonal shift, surprise, or energy change at or near the midpoint to prevent drop-off.
**Metric:** Video completion
**Evidence:** 11 of 13 ads have explicit pace changes documented. The most common interrupt point is between 40-60% of the ad's duration — exactly where completion curves typically cliff.
**Confidence:** Moderate

**Why it works:** Attention naturally decays. A pattern interrupt (music drop, tonal shift, unexpected reveal, new character) resets the attention clock. It signals "something new is happening" which re-engages viewers who were starting to drift.

**Examples:**
- ad_005 (Urban Company): Retro Bollywood music drop at 15s in a 30s ad — sharp tonal shift re-grabs attention.
- ad_010 (Adani): Catchphrase repetition in new contexts keeps restarting the mystery.
- ad_012 (Surf Excel): Parent's initial worried reaction at the midpoint — "will they scold the kid?" — creates tension that demands resolution.

**How to apply:** In a 30s Wiom ad, plan a deliberate energy shift at 12-18 seconds. This could be: the problem getting suddenly worse, a humor beat, a new character entering, or a musical shift. Never let the energy flatline.

## PATTERNS THAT DRIVE CTR

### Pattern K1: Earned CTA — Story Logic Leads to Action

**What:** The CTA feels like the natural conclusion of the narrative, not an appended ask.
**Metric:** CTR
**Evidence:** 12 of 13 ads place CTA late (60%+ of duration). But the key distinction: in the strongest ads, the narrative structure makes the CTA feel inevitable. The story leads TO the action.
**Confidence:** Moderate

**Why it works:** A CTA that follows a compelling story feels like the viewer's own idea, not the brand's demand. "I just watched a family struggle with bad internet for 25 seconds — of course I want to fix that" is more powerful than "click here for broadband."

**Examples:**
- ad_009 (Sony LIV): Reverse psychology means the viewer WANTS to defy the ad's instruction. CTA (apply now) feels like rebellion, not compliance.
- ad_011 (Meesho): Festive shopping context means the audience is already in buying mode. CTA pushes an open door.
- ad_008 (Colgate): Behavioral CTA (brush at night) is the logical conclusion of "Colgate sells food because you eat at night."

**Anti-pattern:** Disconnected CTAs that could be appended to any ad ("Visit our website!"). If the CTA does not reference the narrative, it breaks the spell.

**How to apply:** Build Wiom ads so the final 5 seconds feel like the only logical conclusion. If the ad shows a family suffering from bad internet, the CTA "Switch to Wiom" is earned. If the ad shows kids acing an online exam, the CTA "Give your kids Wiom" is earned. The story does the selling; the CTA just opens the door.

---

### Pattern K2: Sound-Off Survivability

**What:** Ads designed to communicate their core message visually, without audio.
**Metric:** CTR (especially Meta/Instagram feed)
**Evidence:** 6 of 13 ads use minimal text overlay, and 3 specifically have visual-comedy or visual-narrative that works without sound (ad_002 Lahori Zeera, ad_003 WD-40, ad_009 Sony LIV with heavy text). The dialogue-driven ads (ad_004 Zomato, ad_007 Cadbury, ad_013 Tata Tea) explicitly DO NOT work sound-off.
**Confidence:** Emerging

**Why it works:** 85% of Meta feed videos are watched without sound. An ad that requires audio to communicate its message loses 85% of potential impressions. Visual storytelling or bold text overlays bridge the gap.

**Two approaches:**
1. **Visual comedy** (Lahori Zeera, WD-40): The humor and story are in the visuals. Audio is a bonus, not a requirement.
2. **Text-as-content** (Sony LIV): Bold typography carries the message. The text IS the visual.

**How to apply:** For Meta/Instagram placements, Wiom ads MUST work with sound off. Test by watching without audio — if the core message is unclear, add text overlays or redesign the visual narrative. For YouTube (sound-on default), dialogue-driven approaches are fine.

## PATTERNS THAT DRIVE APP DOWNLOADS / BOOKINGS

### Pattern D1: Commodity-to-Emotion Reframe

**What:** Transform a commodity product into an emotional choice by attaching meaning beyond functional specs.
**Metric:** Downloads / Bookings (through brand preference, not direct response)
**Evidence:** 5 of 13 ads explicitly reframe a commodity: ad_012 (stains = good values), ad_006 (investing = daily life), ad_001 (broadband = entertainment access), ad_010 (electricity = progress), ad_007 (chocolate = human connection). These are the ads that build long-term brand preference ��� the kind that drives choice when a family is deciding which ISP to pick.
**Confidence:** Moderate

**Why it works:** When products are functionally interchangeable (all broadband is "fast internet"), the purchase decision shifts to emotional territory: "which brand do I trust / feel good about / identify with?" Reframing the product's meaning gives the brand an emotional moat competitors cannot copy.

**Examples:**
- ad_012 (Surf Excel): Detergent is a commodity. "Daag Acche Hain" makes Surf Excel the brand that celebrates childhood, not just cleans clothes. Parents CHOOSE Surf Excel because they buy the philosophy, not the formula.
- ad_006 (HDFC MF): Mutual funds are intimidating. "We Are All Investors" makes HDFC the brand that respects your intelligence. Consumers choose HDFC because it made them feel smart, not stupid.

**How to apply:** Wiom must find its emotional reframe. Broadband is "just internet" until you make it mean something:
- "Wiom = the connection that keeps your family together" (warmth)
- "Wiom = every kid deserves fast internet to learn" (equity/pride)
- "Wiom = internet that works so you can live, not troubleshoot" (relief)
Pick one. Own it. Build every ad around it.

### Pattern D2: Family as Protagonist

**What:** Indian video ads that place the family unit (parents + kids, 4-5 members) at the center outperform individual-focused ads for household products.
**Metric:** Downloads / Bookings (for household products/services)
**Evidence:** 6 of 13 ads use family dynamics: ad_011 (Meesho: family shopping), ad_012 (Surf Excel: parent-child), ad_013 (Tata Tea: generational debate), ad_007 (Cadbury: neighborhood/community), ad_001 (Airtel: family entertainment), ad_010 (Adani: village/community). All household-product ads in the library use family framing.
**Confidence:** Moderate

**Why it works:** Broadband is a household purchase made by parents for the family. The decision-maker (parent) needs to see their family reflected in the ad. Kids create emotional stakes ("I want this for my children"). The family living room is the natural stage for a home internet product.

**How to apply:** Wiom's primary creative territory should be the family of 4-5 in a Tier 2-3 Indian household. Show the parents, show the kids, show them using internet together AND separately. The Wiom router sitting in the background of a living room should become as iconic as the Surf Excel stain.

### Pattern D3: Celebrity as Voice, Not Face

**What:** Celebrity endorsements work when the celebrity embodies the brand voice, not just lends their face.
**Metric:** Downloads (through attention + trust transfer)
**Evidence:** 4 of 13 ads use celebrities: ad_004 (Piyush Mishra for Zomato's satire), ad_011 (Kapil Sharma for Meesho's mass comedy), ad_001 (entertainment characters for Airtel). In each case, the celebrity's persona MATCHES the brand's tone. No ad in the library uses a celebrity doing a generic endorsement.
**Confidence:** Emerging

**Why it works:** A generic celebrity endorsement triggers ad-skepticism ("they're being paid to say this"). A celebrity whose persona aligns with the brand voice creates authentic-feeling communication. Kapil Sharma for Meesho works because he IS lower-middle-class humor personified.

**How to apply:** If Wiom uses celebrity, choose someone whose persona matches Wiom's brand voice. For a family-focused ISP in Tier 2-3: a TV actor known for family roles, a comedian known for relatable middle-class humor, or a local influencer with regional credibility. Never a Bollywood A-lister doing a generic "Wiom is fast!" read.

## ANTI-PATTERNS — WHAT DOESN'T WORK

### Anti-Pattern X1: Brand-First Opening
**What:** Opening with logo animation, brand jingle, or brand name before any story/hook.
**Evidence:** 0 of 13 best-in-class ads open with a logo animation. Even the 3 with early brand presence (WD-40, Colgate, Sony LIV) use the brand as part of a creative surprise, not as a standalone introduction.
**Why it fails:** Signals "ad" before the viewer is engaged. Triggers skip/scroll.
**Severity:** High — this is the single most common mistake in ISP advertising.

### Anti-Pattern X2: Speed-Test Advertising
**What:** ISP ads that lead with speed numbers, tech specs, or buffer-wheel imagery.
**Evidence:** ad_001 (Airtel Xstream) is the only ISP ad in the library — it explicitly NEVER mentions speed. It sells entertainment. Every successful commodity-to-emotion ad (C1, D1) avoids functional specs in favor of emotional meaning.
**Why it fails:** Speed is a commodity claim every ISP makes. It does not differentiate. It does not create emotional connection. Families do not dream about megabits.
**Severity:** High for Wiom specifically.

### Anti-Pattern X3: Flat Energy / No Pattern Interrupt
**What:** Ads that maintain the same tone, pace, and energy throughout without any shift.
**Evidence:** All 13 ads in the library have documented pace changes and pattern interrupts. Flat energy = predictable = boring = drop-off.
**Why it fails:** The brain habituates to constant stimuli. Without change, attention decays.
**Severity:** Medium.

### Anti-Pattern X4: Generic CTA ("Learn More" / "Visit Website")
**What:** CTAs that could be appended to any ad from any brand.
**Evidence:** The strongest CTAs in the library are narrative-connected: "Apply now" (after being told not to), "Order this Diwali" (after cultural moment), "Brush tonight" (after food comedy). None of the top ads use generic CTAs.
**Why it fails:** A generic CTA breaks the narrative spell. It reminds the viewer they are watching an ad, reducing the emotional momentum that drives action.
**Severity:** Medium.

### Anti-Pattern X5: Cultural Tone-Deafness
**What:** Ads that miss the cultural register of their target audience (too urban for Tier 3, too English for Hindi belt, too preachy for youth).
**Evidence:** The strongest culturally-specific ads (Meesho, Tata Tea, Surf Excel) succeed because they PRECISELY match the cultural register of their audience. Meesho is pure Hindi, festive, family-focused, value-messaging — exactly right for Tier 2-3 households.
**Why it fails:** Cultural mismatch creates distance. The viewer thinks "this is not for me" and disengages.
**Severity:** High for Wiom targeting Tier 2-3 India.

## PLATFORM-SPECIFIC NOTES

### Meta (Reels, In-Feed Video)
- Sound-off design is mandatory (Pattern K2)
- 30s or shorter optimal
- Bold text overlays for value/deal messaging
- Visual comedy > dialogue comedy
- First frame must earn the stop-scroll

### YouTube (Pre-Roll, Shorts)
- Sound-on is default — dialogue/music-driven approaches work
- 6s bumpers for non-skippable frequency
- 30s for skippable — hook must land in first 3s before skip button appears
- 60s for high-engagement storytelling (requires strong retention mechanic)

### General
- Cut the same creative into platform-specific versions (15s for Reels, 30s for Feed, 6s bumper for YouTube pre-roll) rather than creating one-size-fits-all

## INDIA-SPECIFIC NOTES

### Language
- 11 of 13 ads use Hindi or pure Hindi. 1 uses heavy Hinglish code-switching (Zomato). 0 use English-first.
- For Tier 2-3 India, pure Hindi is the default. Hinglish works for urban/millennial audiences.
- Regional language versions are table-stakes, not optional (Airtel does 9 languages).

### Cultural Patterns
- **Festival timing** is a multiplier, not optional (Meesho, Zomato). Diwali, Onam, Eid, Pongal.
- **Family dynamics** are the dominant emotional territory for household products.
- **Generational tension** (parents vs kids) is a rich vein — Tata Tea and Surf Excel both mine it.
- **Aspiration vs Relatability**: lower-middle-class audiences respond to "I can afford nice things" (Meesho) more than "look at this aspirational lifestyle."

### Regional
- Library is currently Hindi-belt heavy. Gaps: Tamil, Telugu, Kannada, Bengali markets.
- Regional ads may follow different patterns — flag for future library expansion.

## SCORING RUBRIC

Use this rubric when scoring Wiom ads with Agent R. Each dimension scored 1-5.

### Hook Score (Weight: 30%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5** | Opens with a relatable moment, visual surprise, or curiosity gap. No brand in first 3s (unless brand IS the surprise). Face or action in first frame. Viewer MUST watch the next 3 seconds. Matches Pattern C1 + C2. |
| **4** | Strong hook that earns attention. Minor issue (e.g., slightly slow setup, brand appears early without creative justification). |
| **3** | Decent hook but does not match any top pattern. Functional but not compelling. Viewer COULD scroll past. |
| **2** | Weak hook. Slow start, unclear what the ad is about. Partially matches an anti-pattern (brand-first, speed-test imagery). |
| **1** | Logo animation opener. Speed/spec claims first. No human element. Matches Anti-Pattern X1 or X2. |

### Retention Score (Weight: 25%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5** | Uses curiosity engine (C2) — open loop, escalation, or debate. Pattern interrupt at midpoint (C4). Pace changes documented. No flat energy zone. Viewer watches to the end to resolve the tension. |
| **4** | Strong retention with clear pace changes. Minor flat zone but recovers. |
| **3** | Watchable but no compelling reason to stay past 50%. No pattern interrupt. |
| **2** | Flat energy. Predictable structure. Viewer likely drops at 15s. |
| **1** | No retention mechanic. Same energy throughout. Matches Anti-Pattern X3. |

### CTR Driver Score (Weight: 20%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5** | Story logically leads to action (K1). Works sound-off for Meta (K2). CTA is narrative-connected, not generic. Creates desire/urgency through story, not just offer. |
| **4** | Strong drivers but one gap (e.g., sound-off doesn't fully work, or CTA slightly disconnected). |
| **3** | Functional. Product is clear, CTA exists, but no narrative drive toward action. |
| **2** | Product unclear until end. CTA is generic. Does not work sound-off. |
| **1** | No clear reason to click. CTA is an afterthought. Matches Anti-Pattern X4. |

### CTA/Conversion Score (Weight: 15%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5** | CTA is specific to campaign objective (download app / book now). Timing is earned by narrative. Urgency element present (festival, scarcity, social proof). Friction is minimized ("download free" not "sign up and create account"). |
| **4** | Good CTA with minor issues (e.g., slightly late timing, weak urgency). |
| **3** | Generic but present. "Learn more" or "Visit site" without specificity. |
| **2** | CTA present but disconnected or buried. No urgency. |
| **1** | No CTA or completely generic. "Visit our website" with no context. |

### Brand Coherence Score (Weight: 10%)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| **5** | Brand has a distinctive voice/aesthetic (like Urban Company's retro, Zomato's wit, Surf Excel's warmth). Brand presence enhances the creative. Logo placement is intentional and earns its moment. |
| **4** | Consistent brand voice. Logo placement appropriate. Minor inconsistency. |
| **3** | Brand is present but generic. Could be any competitor's ad with a logo swap. |
| **2** | Brand feels forced or interrupts the creative flow. |
| **1** | No brand coherence. Logo slapped on at the end of an unrelated creative. No distinctive voice. |
